# Vachan AI

Vachan AI is a dialect-aware Telugu foundation model engineered to understand, generate, and reason across regional and conversational variations of Telugu.

Unlike most existing Telugu language models trained primarily on standardized written corpora, Vachan AI explicitly models dialect diversity, slang variation, and real-world code-mixed usage.

This work is part of our broader research at Nexora AI Labs to build culturally grounded, region-aware AI systems for low-resource languages.

---

## 1. Motivation

Telugu is spoken by more than 80 million people. However, real-world Telugu is not monolithic. It varies significantly across:

- Telangana  
- Coastal Andhra  
- Rayalaseema  

These variations affect:

- Vocabulary  
- Morphology  
- Tone  
- Pragmatics  
- Slang  
- Code-mixing frequency  

Most multilingual LLMs treat Telugu as a standardized written language. As a result:

- Informal conversations degrade in quality  
- Regional idioms are misinterpreted  
- Slang is treated as noise  
- Code-mixed inputs reduce coherence  

Vachan AI aims to close this gap.

---

## 2. Problem Definition

We define dialect-aware language modeling as learning:

P(text | dialect, context)

Where:

dialect ∈ {Telangana, Coastal, Rayalaseema, Neutral}

Objectives:

1. Improve conversational coherence across dialects  
2. Preserve linguistic authenticity  
3. Enhance slang understanding  
4. Improve robustness to code-mixed inputs  
5. Enable controllable regional style generation  

---

## 3. Dataset Construction

### 3.1 Corpus Overview

Total corpus:

- 7.8M sentences  
- ~620M tokens  

Dialect distribution:

| Region | Percentage |
|--------|------------|
| Telangana | 38% |
| Coastal Andhra | 34% |
| Rayalaseema | 28% |

Code-mixed Telugu-English samples: 22%

Average sentence length: 17.4 tokens

---

### 3.2 Data Sources

Data was curated from:

- Regional news websites  
- YouTube transcripts  
- Public interviews  
- Podcasts  
- Public social media conversations  
- Community discussion forums  

All sources were filtered using:

- Language identification models  
- Profanity normalization  
- Deduplication pipelines  
- Noise filtering heuristics  

---

### 3.3 Dialect Annotation Pipeline

Dialect labels were assigned using:

1. Source metadata  
2. Lexical region markers  
3. Region-specific vocabulary dictionaries  
4. Rule-based classifier
